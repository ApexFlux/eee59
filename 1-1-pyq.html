1st Year EEE PYQ Study Guide

Your 5-Course Prioritized Topic Analysis (2021-2024)

Overall Insight
Your teacher builds this course around a set of core, repeating principles. The Incourse exams test the fundamental DC techniques, and the Final exams re-test those exact same techniques but apply them to more complex AC (phasor) circuits.

Tier 1: The "Must-Know" Topics (Highest Priority)

These topics appear on almost every single exam. Mastering these is non-negotiable.

  • The "Big Three" Theorems:
    • Thevenin's Theorem: The teacher's absolute favorite.
    • Superposition Theorem: A very close second, used for both DC and AC.
    • Norton's Theorem: Also frequent, often as an alternative to Thevenin.
  • Mesh (Loop) Analysis: The go-to method for solving complex circuits in the exams.
  • The "Dependent Source" Trick: The teacher loves to include dependent sources (e.g., $2i_x$ or $3v_x$) in all of the "Big Three" and Mesh/Nodal problems.
  • Maximum Power Transfer Theorem: A recurring question. You are asked to state the theorem, prove the 50% efficiency, and find the load ($Z_L$) for max power.

Tier 2: Core Final Exam Topics (High Priority)

These are the foundation of the Final exams.

  • AC Phasor Analysis: This is the evolution of all Tier 1 topics. You must re-do Thevenin, Superposition, and Mesh analysis using complex numbers (impedance).
  • First-Order Transients (RC & RL Circuits): Find $v_c(t)$ or $i_L(t)$ for a circuit with a switch that moves at $t=0$.
  • Three-Phase Circuits: Solving balanced Y-Y or Y-Œî systems to find line currents and voltages.
  • Nodal Analysis: The counterpart to Mesh. Be prepared for supernodes.
  • Equivalent Resistance/Impedance (Req / Zeq): Often requires Y-Œî (Wye-Delta) Transformation to solve.

Tier 3: Supporting Topics (Medium Priority)

These topics round out the exams.

  • AC Power (Power Factor, Complex Power): Calculating P, Q, and S, and power factor correction.
  • RLC Circuits & Resonance: Finding the natural response ($s_1, s_2$) and resonant frequency, bandwidth, and Q-factor.
  • Filters: Basic low-pass RC filter design.
  • RMS Value: Defining and calculating the RMS value.
üí° Overall Suggestions and Study Plan
  1. Start with DC: Do not look at AC circuits yet.
  2. Master the "Big Three" + Mesh/Nodal: Practice DC Thevenin, Norton, Superposition, and Mesh/Nodal.
  3. Add Dependent Sources: Re-do all those problems, but only using examples with dependent sources. This is the most important skill.
  4. Memorize Theory: Memorize the proof for Max Power Transfer (50% efficiency) and the derivation for Y-Œî transformation.
  5. Move to AC: "Upgrade" your DC knowledge to the AC phasor domain. Practice Thevenin, Mesh, and Superposition all over again with complex impedance: $R$, $j\omega L$, $\frac{1}{j\omega C}$.
  6. Practice Transients: Focus on 1st-order RC and RL circuits with switches.
  7. Final Topics: Finish with Three-Phase systems and AC Power Factor calculations.
Overall Insight
This is a "classic" physics paper. The teacher is far more interested in your ability to derive the fundamental equations from first principles than in your ability to solve complex numerical problems.

Tier 1: The "Must-Know" Derivations (Highest Priority)

These are the absolute core of the course and appear on virtually every single Final Exam.

  • Gauss's Law & Its Applications: This is the most important topic.
    • The Derivation: State Gauss's Law and use it to derive the E-field for: an infinite line of charge, a non-conducting sphere, and an infinite plane.
    • The Theory: Be able to show how Coulomb's Law can be derived from Gauss's Law.
  • Electric Dipole: This is the teacher's second-favorite topic.
    • The Derivation: Derive the expression for the electric field ($E$) and/or the electric potential ($V$) at a point (on the axis, on the bisector, or at a general point P).
    • The Theory: Derive the torque ($\tau$) and potential energy ($U$) for a dipole in an external E-field.
  • Capacitance Derivations: A guaranteed high-mark question.
    • The Derivations: Derive the capacitance ($C$) for: a parallel plate, a spherical, and a cylindrical capacitor.
    • The Theory: Derive the energy stored in a capacitor ($U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2$).

Tier 2: Core Magnetism & Circuits (High Priority)

These topics form the second half of the course.

  • Ampere's Law & Biot-Savart Law: State the laws and use them to derive the B-field for: a long straight wire, a solenoid, a toroid, and the center of a circular coil.
  • Hall Effect: Explain the Hall Effect and show how it is used to find the carrier concentration ($n$).
  • RL & LC Circuits (Theory): Derive the expression for current in a charging RL circuit and the oscillation frequency for an LC circuit ($\omega = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}$).
  • Faraday's & Lenz's Law: You must be able to state and explain both laws.

Tier 3: Supporting Concepts (Medium Priority)

These are the "smaller" questions that fill out the exams.

  • Coulomb's Law Calculations: Simple problems (e.g., protons in a nucleus).
  • Electric Potential: Calculating the potential energy of a system of point charges.
  • Equivalent Capacitance: Simple series/parallel calculations.
  • Ohm's Law (Microscopic): Deriving the microscopic form of resistivity ($\rho = \frac{m}{e^2 n \tau}$).
üí° Overall Suggestions and Study Plan
  1. Prioritize Derivations: Do not just memorize the final formulas. Write out the full derivation for every item in Tier 1 and Tier 2 by hand.
  2. Make a "Law" Sheet: Write down the exact definitions for Gauss's Law, Biot-Savart Law, Ampere's Law, Faraday's Law, and Lenz's Law.
  3. Practice the Dipole: The E-field/Potential derivation for a general point (P) on a dipole is complex, but it's a favorite. Master it.
  4. Don't Over-focus on Calculations: The calculations are almost always straightforward. The real marks are in the derivations.
Overall Insight
This is not a "plug-and-chug" calculus course. Your teacher is focused on proofs, specific theorems, and complex differentiation/integration techniques. They consistently ask for the same set of difficult problems.

Tier 1: The "Must-Know" Topics (Highest Priority)

These are the most critical, high-mark topics. They appear on almost every single Final Exam.

  • Leibnitz's Theorem (n-th Derivative): This is the absolute favorite topic.
    • The Problem: Given a complex function (e.g., $y = (x + \sqrt{x^2+1})^m$ or $y = \cos(m \cdot \cos^{-1}x)$), you must prove a relationship involving $y_{n+2}$, $y_{n+1}$, and $y_n$.
    • Strategy: Practice this exact problem type. The steps are always: find $y_1$, find $y_2$, create a differential equation, then apply Leibnitz's Theorem.
  • Continuity & Differentiability Analysis: A guaranteed "Part 1" question.
    • The Problem: Given a piecewise function (e.g., $f(x) = x^2 \sin(1/x)$ at $x=0$), you must check for continuity and differentiability.
    • Strategy: You must use the formal "first principles" (limit definition of the derivative) to prove this.
  • Euler's Theorem (Homogeneous Functions): A recurring proof-based question.
    • The Problem: Given a function (e.g., $u = \frac{x^{1/4}+y^{1/4}}{x^{1/5}+y^{1/5}}$ or $u = \sin^{-1}(\frac{x^2+y^2}{x+y})$), state Euler's theorem and verify it or prove a related relationship like $x\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + y\frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = \sin(2u)$.

Tier 2: Core Final Exam Topics (High Priority)

These topics are the foundation of the rest of the exam.

  • Function Analysis & Curve Sketching: A very common 10-14 mark question. Find everything: intervals of increase/decrease, concave up/down, extrema, inflection points, and asymptotes.
  • Gamma & Beta Functions: The main integration topic.
    • The Problem: Prove the relationship $\beta(m,n) = \frac{\Gamma(m)\Gamma(n)}{\Gamma(m+n)}$ and the related "Wallis's Formula" for $\int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^p\theta \cos^q\theta \,d\theta$.
    • Strategy: Memorize these proofs.
  • Integration as Limit of a Sum (Riemann Sum): Evaluate a complex limit by first converting it into a definite integral.
  • Jacobians: Show that the Jacobian for a change of variables (e.g., Cartesian to Spherical $\frac{\partial(x,y,z)}{\partial(\rho,\theta,\varphi)}$) equals a specific value (like $\rho^2 \sin\theta$).
  • Maclaurin/Taylor Series: State the theorems and use a polynomial to approximate a value (e.g., $\sqrt{e}$) to a decimal place.

Tier 3: Supporting Topics (Medium Priority)

Common in Incourse exams and fill in the gaps on Finals.

  • Graph Transformations (Incourse): Given $f(x)$, sketch $y = 1 - f(x+2)$.
  • Finding Area/Volume: Area between curves, area of a cycloid, volume of a sphere.
  • Multiple Integrals: Evaluating double and triple integrals over a defined region.
üí° Overall Suggestions and Study Plan
  1. Leibnitz's Theorem First: Master this topic. Practice the $y_{n+2}$ proofs until you cannot get them wrong.
  2. Focus on Proofs, Not Just Problems: Create a "Proof Sheet" for Leibnitz, Euler, Beta-Gamma Relationship ($\beta(m,n) = \dots$), Wallis's Formula, and the Jacobian.
  3. Master Piecewise Functions: Practice the formal limit definition of continuity and differentiability, especially for $x^2 \sin(1/x)$.
  4. Practice Curve Sketching: Develop a rigid, step-by-step procedure for the "Function Analysis" questions.
Overall Insight
This course is a tale of two subjects: Linear Algebra (The "Theory") and Numerical Analysis (The "Methods"). The teacher tests application by asking you to set up real-world problems (circuits, traffic) as linear systems.

Tier 1: The "Must-Know" Topics (Highest Priority)

These topics appear on every final exam in some form.

  • Solving Systems of Linear Equations: The #1 most important skill.
    • Gaussian Elimination: The primary method.
    • Application Problems: You must be able to look at an electrical circuit or traffic network, set up the linear equations, and solve.
    • Finding 'a' for Solution Types: Find when a system with a variable 'a' has no, one, or infinitely many solutions.
  • Root Finding Methods (Numerical): You are guaranteed to get one of these.
    • Bisection Method
    • Newton-Raphson Method (Often asks for "invalid initial choices").
  • Eigenvalues and Eigenspaces: A classic final exam question. You must be able to define them, calculate the eigenvalues ($\lambda$), and find the bases for the eigenspaces.
  • Numerical Integration (from a Table): A guaranteed, straightforward question.
    • Trapezoidal Rule
    • Simpson's 1/3 Rule
  • Interpolation (from a Table): Another guaranteed question.
    • Lagrange's Interpolation
    • Newton's Divided Difference
  • Linear Independence (LI): Define it and determine if a set of vectors (in $R^3$ or $P_2$) is LI.

Tier 2: Core Final Exam Topics (High Priority)

Very common topics that round out the core of the exams.

  • Cayley-Hamilton Theorem: State the theorem and use it to find the inverse ($A^{-1}$) of a matrix.
  • Basis and Coordinate Vectors: Show a set forms a basis and find the coordinate vector of 'v' relative to a basis 'S'.
  • Numerical Differentiation (from a Table): Finding velocity and acceleration from a table of distance vs. time.
  • Rank and Nullity: Define and find the rank and nullity of a matrix.
  • Subspaces: Define and prove if a given set W (e.g., $W = \{ A \in M_{22} : a+b+c+d=0 \}$) is a subspace.

Tier 3: Supporting Topics (Medium Priority)

These topics appear less frequently.

  • Solving Systems with Matrix Inverse: Solve a system by first finding $A^{-1}$.
  • Linear Transformations (Kernel/Range): Define and find the basis/dimension for the Kernel and Range.
  • Specialized Numerical Methods: Finite Difference Method, Natural Cubic Spline.
üí° Overall Suggestions and Study Plan
  1. Divide and Conquer: Treat this as two separate courses: Numerical Methods and Linear Algebra.
  2. Master the "Methods" First: The Numerical part is the easiest place to score full marks. Create a "formula sheet" for Bisection, Newton-Raphson, Lagrange, Newton's DD, Trapezoidal, and Simpson's.
  3. Master the "Big 3" LA Skills: (1) Solving Systems (especially circuit/traffic applications), (2) Finding Eigenvalues/Eigenspaces ($\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$), and (3) Checking for Linear Independence.
  4. Memorize LA Proofs & Definitions: Make a sheet for definitions (LI, Subspace, Basis, Eigenvalue) and key proofs (how to prove a subspace, how to use Cayley-Hamilton for $A^{-1}$).
Overall Insight
This course is about memorizing formats and mastering specific grammar rules. The teacher is extremely consistent, testing the exact same formats (complaint letters, CVs) and exact same grammar errors (especially prepositions) year after year.

Tier 1: The "Must-Know" Topics (Highest Priority)

These are the most predictable, high-mark questions.

  • The Complaint Letter: The single most important topic.
    • The Prompt: "You ordered furniture/products, they arrived late, had poor quality, or were the wrong item. Write a complaint letter." This prompt is repeated almost verbatim.
    • Strategy: Memorize a template for this exact prompt. Also know the "dos and don'ts" as a theory question.
  • Reading Comprehension: Every Final Exam has a long passage followed by questions.
  • Correcting Sentences (Prepositions!): The teacher's favorite grammar test. 90% of the errors are wrong prepositions.
    • Afraid of (not "from")
    • Accustomed to (not "with")
    • Believe in (not "to")
    • Angry with (not "against")
    • Congratulate on (not "for")
  • Right Form of Verbs: The other major grammar topic. Review rules for "had better," "with a view to," and "It is high time."

Tier 2: Core Final Exam Topics (High Priority)

These topics are the foundation of the rest of the exam.

  • Essay Writing (Argumentative/Compare-Contrast): The main "long-form" writing task (e.g., "Public vs. Private Universities" or "Rickshaws should be banned").
  • CV & Cover Letter Writing: The other key "Technical Communication" format.
    • The Task: Write a CV/Cover Letter for a specific job (e.g., "Lecturer in EEE at DU").
    • The Theory: You must know the differences between a CV, Resume, and Biodata.
  • Reading Skills (Theory): You will be asked to write short notes on: Skimming, Scanning, and Annotation.
  • Grammar (Conditionals & Voice Change): Completing "If I were you..." sentences and changing from active to passive.

Tier 3: Supporting Topics (Medium Priority)

Mostly from the Incourse or less frequent on Finals.

  • Phonetics (Incourse): Identifying word stress, transcribing words, and defining monophthongs/diphthongs.
  • Summary vs. Pr√©cis: Knowing the difference is a common theory question.
üí° Overall Suggestions and Study Plan
  1. Create a "Cheat Sheet": Your top priority. It must have:
    • A perfect, memorized template for the Complaint Letter.
    • A template for a CV and Cover Letter.
    • A list of the correct prepositions (Afraid of, Accustomed to, etc.).
    • The definitions for CV vs. Resume, Summary vs. Pr√©cis, and Skimming vs. Scanning.
  2. Drill the Grammar: Do all the "Right Form of Verbs," "Correct the Sentence," and "Conditional" exercises from the PYQs. The patterns are identical.
  3. Practice One Essay and One Reading Comp: Time yourself to get the speed right.
  4. Don't Over-Stress Phonetics: This is a minor, early-term topic. Your final grade depends on the Complaint Letter, Essay, and Grammar.
